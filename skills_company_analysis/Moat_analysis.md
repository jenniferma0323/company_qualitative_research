When the user asks about a company's competitive advantage, moat, barriers to entry, or long-term defensibility, use this framework to structure your analysis.

## Step 1: Moat Indicators

**Key question: do the numbers show a moat exists?**

These are quantitative signals that a moat likely exists. A company can score well on some but not all — assess each and weigh the evidence.

### 1.1 Return on Invested Capital (ROIC)

A durable moat should generate returns above the cost of capital over long periods.

| Signal | Interpretation |
|---|---|
| ROIC > 15% consistently (5+ years) | Strong moat — the business earns well above cost of capital |
| ROIC 10–15% consistently | Moderate moat — decent but may face competitive erosion |
| ROIC < 10% or declining | Weak or no moat — capital is not earning excess returns |
| ROIC volatility (big swings year to year) | Cyclical business or moat under pressure |

**How to assess:**
- Review ROIC over ~10 years to see the trend.
- Compare ROIC to the company's estimated WACC (typically 8–12% for most companies). ROIC consistently above WACC = value creation.
- Compare ROIC against key competitors.

### 1.2 Margin Stability vs Peers

A moated company maintains higher and more stable margins than competitors, even during downturns.

| Signal | Interpretation |
|---|---|
| Gross margin 10+ pts above peer median, stable over 5 years | Strong pricing power / cost advantage |
| Operating margin above peers but narrowing | Moat may be eroding — investigate why |
| Margins collapse during recessions then recover | Cyclical, not a moat — the business has no pricing cushion |
| Margins stable during industry downturns | Strong moat — customers don't leave even under pressure |

**How to assess:**
- Review the income statement over ~10 years for the target company.
- Identify 3–5 key competitors.
- Compare gross margin, operating margin, and net margin trends against those peers.
- Look for margin stability (low standard deviation) as much as margin level (high average).

### 1.3 Pricing Power

The ability to raise prices above inflation consistently, without losing customers.

| Signal | Interpretation |
|---|---|
| Revenue grows faster than volume (price/mix driving growth) | Strong pricing power |
| Company explicitly discusses price increases in earnings calls | Direct evidence — check earnings calls |
| Customers absorb price hikes with minimal churn | High switching costs or brand loyalty |
| Revenue declines when prices are raised | No pricing power — commodity business |

**How to assess:**
- Check earnings calls and filings for discussion of pricing actions, price increases, average selling price, and price/mix contribution to revenue growth.
- Review the income statement to check if revenue growth outpaces volume growth over time.
- Check industry sources for pricing trends and inflation benchmarks.

### 1.4 Consistent Market Share

A moated company holds or grows its market share over extended periods.

| Signal | Interpretation |
|---|---|
| Market share stable or growing over 5+ years | Durable competitive position |
| Share gains during industry downturns | Strongest moat signal — weaker competitors lose ground |
| Share gains only during booms, losses during busts | Cyclical advantage, not a structural moat |
| Declining share despite growing revenue | Market is growing faster — the moat may be weakening |

**How to assess:**
- Check earnings calls and 10-K filings for management discussion of market share.
- Review revenue by segment to see if core segments are growing.
- Check third-party market share data and industry reports.
- Compare the company's revenue growth rate to the overall industry growth rate.

## Step 2: Sources of Moat — Barriers to Entry

**Key question: what structural barrier creates the moat?**

These are the structural reasons **why** a moat exists. When analyzing a company, identify which barriers apply and how strong they are.

### 2.1 Network Effects (Strong Form)

The product or service becomes more valuable as more people use it. This is the strongest form of moat when present.

- **Direct network effects**: Each new user adds value for all existing users (e.g., social networks, payment networks, marketplaces).
- **Indirect network effects**: More users attract more complementary providers, which attract more users (e.g., app stores, operating systems).
- **Strength test**: Would the product be nearly useless with only 100 users? If yes, strong network effects.
- **Vulnerability**: Network effects can tip — if users leave, the value drops for everyone. Look for **multi-homing** (users on multiple competing platforms) as a weakening signal.

**How to assess:** Check filings and earnings calls for management discussion of user growth, engagement, and platform dynamics; review user/subscriber metrics and the competitive landscape.

### 2.2 Minimum Efficient Scale (MES)

The market is only large enough to profitably support a small number of competitors. New entrants would make the market unprofitable for everyone.

- Applies when the fixed cost base is high relative to total market size.
- Common in industries like defense, aircraft manufacturing, railroads, telecom infrastructure.
- **Key question**: If a new player entered, would market economics deteriorate for all participants?

**How to assess:** Review the cash-flow statement and income statement for capex and fixed cost structure; check industry size and the number of competitors.

### 2.3 Capacity Build Time

The number of years required to build new production capacity from scratch. Long build times deter new entrants because they must commit capital years before earning any revenue.

- **1–2 years**: Low barrier (software, retail).
- **3–5 years**: Moderate barrier (manufacturing plants, logistics networks).
- **5–10+ years**: High barrier (semiconductor fabs, mining operations, nuclear plants, LNG terminals).
- Consider not just construction time but also regulatory approval, permitting, and ramp-up to full production.

**How to assess:** Check filings for capital expenditure plans and capacity expansion timelines; review industry-specific build timelines.

### 2.4 Licenses & Regulatory Requirements

Government-granted licenses, permits, or regulatory approvals that restrict the number of competitors.

- **Strongest form**: Limited licenses (e.g., banking charters, spectrum licenses, gaming licenses, pharmaceutical patents).
- **Moderate form**: Heavy regulatory burden that creates compliance cost barriers (e.g., financial services, healthcare, nuclear energy).
- **Weak form**: Professional certifications or standards that can be obtained with effort.
- **Risk**: Regulatory moats can be removed by policy changes. Monitor regulatory developments.

**How to assess:** Check 10-K risk factors for discussion of the regulatory environment; review regulatory changes and licensing requirements.

### 2.5 Critical Resource Control

Ownership or control of scarce resources that competitors cannot easily replicate.

- **Natural resources**: Mines, oil/gas reserves, water rights, prime real estate locations.
- **Intellectual property**: Patents, proprietary technology, trade secrets.
- **Talent**: Specialized workforce that takes years to develop (e.g., ASML's EUV lithography engineers).
- **Data**: Proprietary datasets that improve with scale and are difficult to replicate.

**How to assess:** Check 10-K filings for discussion of reserves, patents, and IP portfolio; review the income statement for R&D spending trends; check patent counts and resource ownership.

### 2.6 Long-Term Contracts

Multi-year contractual agreements that lock in revenue and lock out competitors.

- **Strength indicators**: Average contract duration, % of revenue under contract, renewal rates.
- **Strongest when**: Contracts include price escalators, early termination penalties, and exclusivity clauses.
- **Weakest when**: Contracts are short-term, easily cancellable, or won through lowest-price bidding.

**How to assess:** Check earnings calls and 10-K filings for backlog, contracted revenue, and customer retention discussion; review the income statement for revenue stability.

### 2.7 Customer Captivity / Switching Costs

Costs (financial, operational, or psychological) that customers face when switching to a competitor.

- **Financial switching costs**: Contractual penalties, retraining costs, data migration costs.
- **Operational switching costs**: Integration complexity, workflow disruption, productivity loss during transition.
- **Psychological switching costs**: Brand loyalty, trust, familiarity, habit.
- **Strongest signal**: High customer retention rates (>90%) and low churn despite competitors offering lower prices.

**How to assess:** Check earnings calls for churn rates, retention rates, and customer lifetime value; review competitive comparisons and customer satisfaction data.

### 2.8 Excess Capacity

Incumbent firms hold excess production capacity that can be deployed to flood the market and drive down prices if a new entrant appears.

- Acts as a credible threat against new competitors — "if you enter, we'll overproduce and crash prices."
- Common in commodity industries: steel, chemicals, airlines, cement.
- **Assess**: Compare the company's capacity utilization rate to its total capacity. Low utilization + profitability = deliberate excess capacity as a deterrent.

**How to assess:** Check filings for capacity utilization and production volumes; review the income statement for fixed cost leverage patterns.

### 2.9 Learning Curves & Know-How

Accumulated experience drives down costs over time. Late entrants face a permanent cost disadvantage.

- **Strongest in**: Complex manufacturing (semiconductors, aerospace), specialized services, process-intensive industries.
- **Measure**: Does the company's cost per unit decline predictably with cumulative production volume?
- **Vulnerability**: Learning curves can be leapfrogged by disruptive technology.

**How to assess:** Review the income statement for cost trends and gross margin improvement over time; check filings and earnings calls for management discussion of yield improvements and process optimization.

### 2.10 Economies of Scale

Larger producers have lower per-unit costs, making it difficult for smaller competitors to match pricing.

- **Cost-side scale**: Spreading fixed costs (R&D, marketing, infrastructure) over larger volumes.
- **Revenue-side scale**: Bargaining power with suppliers and distributors.
- **Assess**: Compare gross margins and operating margins across different-sized competitors. If larger companies have structurally higher margins, scale economies are at work.
- **Limitation**: Scale advantages plateau — after a certain size, further growth doesn't meaningfully lower costs.

**How to assess:** Review the income statement to compare margin structures across competitors of different sizes; review revenue by segment for segment-level economies.

### 2.11 High Initial Capital Requirements

The total upfront investment required to enter the market at a competitive scale.

- **Assess**: What is the minimum investment (capex + working capital + marketing + R&D) needed to reach minimum efficient scale?
- **Stronger when**: Capital is sunk (non-recoverable if the venture fails), not just large.
- **Examples**: Semiconductor fabs ($10B+), telecom networks ($B+), automotive manufacturing ($B+), pharmaceutical R&D ($1B+ per drug).

**How to assess:** Review the cash-flow statement for capex patterns; check greenfield investment costs in the industry.

### 2.12 Data Flywheel

A self-reinforcing loop where usage generates proprietary data, that data improves the product (better models, personalization, recommendations, pricing, risk scoring), the better product attracts more usage, and more usage generates still more data. Unlike the static "data" sub-point under Critical Resource Control (2.5), the moat here is the **compounding loop**, not the dataset itself — the advantage widens automatically with scale and is extremely hard for a late entrant to replicate even with capital.

- **Loop test**: Does more usage *directly* make the product measurably better for all users, without manual intervention? If yes, a true flywheel exists.
- **Strength indicators**: Proprietary (not purchasable) data, data that is fresh/continuously regenerated, a tight feedback loop (data improves the product on short cycles), and improvements that are hard to reverse-engineer.
- **Distinguish from network effects**: Network effects come from users connecting to *each other*; a data flywheel comes from the *aggregate data* improving the product, even if users never interact. Many strong moats (search, recommendation engines, fraud detection, autonomous driving, ad targeting) have both.
- **Vulnerability**: Diminishing returns to data (the product stops improving past a certain data volume), commoditization of the underlying models, synthetic data or foundation models lowering the data advantage, and privacy/regulatory limits on data collection.

**How to assess:** Check earnings calls and 10-K filings for management discussion of proprietary data, model improvement, personalization, and "data advantage"; review competitive data-scale comparisons and discussion of whether the data advantage is durable or commoditizing.
